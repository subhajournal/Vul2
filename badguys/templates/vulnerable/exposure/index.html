Sensitive Data Exposure

{% endblock %} {% block content %}

There are basically two forms of exposure problems: failure to protect data at rest, and failure to protect data in transit.

"Data at rest" is what you might have in your database, just sitting around. It can be payment information or login credentials that aren't encrypted, or are too-weakly encrypted. Maybe they're hashed, but not correctly salted (LinkedIn), or they're MD5s (Evernote) when they should be something stronger like SHA-1 or SHA-2 (Evernote). You can also have issues storing your encryption keys or other secret values in insecure ways.

"Data in transit" is what you're sending across the wire. Problems here can result from failing to secure the connection between endpoints when sensitive data is being transmitted or received. This could be a login form that doesn't POST over HTTPS, or an unsecured payment page that leaks your credit card. It could be a session cookie that's sent in the clear and thus easy pickings for anyone else at the coffee shop to steal and use to impersonate you (go look up "Firesheep" and be very afraid). It could even be a password recovery feature that emails your password to you in clear text! Problems can also be caused by using too-weak encryption or having an invalid or expired SSL certificate.

We're omitting actual exercises here because proxies like Charles and Wireshark won't see traffic going to 127.0.0.1/localhost.
